Success Story

More than a Network:
PPHSN as a Pacific Strategic Asset

How a Pacific-owned surveillance network grew into a regional engine for health security — adapting to new threats while staying country-led.

Reading the Current

Four chapters of one regional story

01
The Challenge & Structural Context

The waters were changing

A network built for one era now faced threats moving faster, and on more fronts, than ever before.

  • Rising public health threats linked to communicable diseases, NCDs and climate change.
  • A network that needed to be both expansive and adaptive.
  • Country-owned and country-managed at its core.
  • A regional network expected to stay responsive to each country's needs.
  • Persistent financial challenges and questions of long-term sustainability.
02
The Strategic Intervention & Actions

Charting a new course

Rather than patch the old structure, the team redrew the map — governance, strategy and service arms together.

  • A full review of the governance structure.
  • A strategic framework, costed implementation plan and theory of change.
  • Evolution of the service arms — including the launch of the Pacific Vector Network (PVN).
  • Phased expansion of PSSS to multi-source surveillance system.
  • Development of a regional mechanism for deployment and response.
03
The Measurable Outcome

Wind in the sails

The shift showed up where it matters most — in capability that countries now own and lead.

  • Improved surveillance and response, evidenced by SHIP-DDM and entomology training evaluations.
  • SHIP-DDM improvement projects delivered across the region.
  • SHIP-DDM graduates now leading health-security initiatives in their own countries.
  • Laboratory (LabNet) and infection-control (PICNet) services strengthened.
  • Vector surveillance and control strengthened.
  • SPC, a regional intergovernmental organisation owned by its member countries, functions as the PPHSN Focal Point.
04
Foundational Takeaway & Values

Why it matters

Beneath the metrics sits a simple principle that holds the whole network together.

  • Countries are able to meet IHR core competencies and to prepare for and respond to public health threats and outbreaks.
  • Surveillance and response are better managed when they are shared.
  • PPHSN is not perfect, it is under-resourced, but it is operational and is trusted by its members.
The Fleet

Seven networks, one mission

PPHSN works through seven service networks that together prevent, detect and respond to epidemics across the Pacific.

Capacity building

Strengthening Health Interventions in the Pacific — building core epidemiology skills through ‘from work, at work, for work’ training.

Outbreak detection

An early-warning tool tracking outbreak-prone syndromes weekly across countries in the region.

Alert & communication

The PPHSN listserv connecting more than 1,000 health professionals for rapid alerts and shared resources.

Preparedness & response

The response arm — multidisciplinary outbreak teams in every country and territory, maintaining surveillance and response protocols.

Vector management

The seventh service network, scaling up coordinated vector management across the Pacific Island Countries and Territories.

Verification & identification

A three-tier laboratory network — from national labs to regional and reference centres — for diagnostic confirmation.

Infection control

The infection prevention and control network, building a qualified IPC workforce across the blue Pacific.
